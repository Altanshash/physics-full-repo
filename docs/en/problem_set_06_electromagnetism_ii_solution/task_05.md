# Problem 5 – Induction in a Moving Rod

## Problem Statement

We have a rod of length $L = 0.25\ \text{m}$ moving with velocity $v = 4\ \text{m/s}$ perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field:

$$
\vec{B} = 0.6\ \text{T}
$$

Answer the following questions:

1. Determine $\mathcal{E}$.
2. Determine the potential difference between the ends.
3. What will happen if the motion is not perpendicular to $\vec{B}$?
4. How does $\mathcal{E}$ depend on $L$?
5. Where does the energy of the electric field in the rod come from?

---

## Given

$$
L = 0.25\ \text{m}
$$

$$
v = 4\ \text{m/s}
$$

$$
B = 0.6\ \text{T}
$$

The rod moves perpendicular to the magnetic field:

$$
\theta = 90^\circ
$$

---

## Solution

## 1. Induced EMF

For a rod moving in a magnetic field, the induced EMF is:

$$
\mathcal{E} = BLv\sin\theta
$$

Since the motion is perpendicular to the magnetic field:

$$
\sin 90^\circ = 1
$$

So:

$$
\mathcal{E} = BLv
$$

Substitute the values:

$$
\mathcal{E} = 0.6 \cdot 0.25 \cdot 4
$$

$$
\mathcal{E} = 0.6\ \text{V}
$$

---

## 2. Potential Difference Between the Ends

The potential difference between the ends of the rod is equal to the induced EMF:

$$
\Delta V = \mathcal{E}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\Delta V = 0.6\ \text{V}
$$

---

## 3. If Motion Is Not Perpendicular to Magnetic Field

If the motion is not perpendicular to $\vec{B}$, then only the perpendicular component of velocity creates EMF.

The general formula is:

$$
\mathcal{E} = BLv\sin\theta
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$.

If $\theta$ decreases, then $\sin\theta$ also decreases.

So the induced EMF becomes smaller.

If the motion is parallel to the magnetic field:

$$
\theta = 0^\circ
$$

$$
\sin 0^\circ = 0
$$

Therefore:

$$
\mathcal{E} = 0
$$

---

## 4. Dependence on Length

The induced EMF is:

$$
\mathcal{E} = BLv
$$

So:

$$
\mathcal{E} \propto L
$$

This means EMF is directly proportional to the length of the rod.

If the length becomes twice larger:

$$
L \rightarrow 2L
$$

then:

$$
\mathcal{E} \rightarrow 2\mathcal{E}
$$

---

## 5. Source of Energy

The energy of the electric field in the rod comes from the mechanical work done to move the rod.

When the rod moves through the magnetic field, charges inside the rod experience the magnetic force:

$$
\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})
$$

This force separates positive and negative charges.

As a result, an electric field appears inside the rod.

To keep the rod moving, an external force must do work.

So the electric energy comes from mechanical energy.

---

## Final Conclusion

The induced EMF is:

$$
\mathcal{E} = BLv
$$

For the given values:

$$
\mathcal{E} = 0.6\ \text{V}
$$

The potential difference between the ends of the rod is:

$$
\Delta V = 0.6\ \text{V}
$$

If the motion is not perpendicular to $\vec{B}$, then:

$$
\mathcal{E} = BLv\sin\theta
$$

The EMF is directly proportional to the length of the rod:

$$
\mathcal{E} \propto L
$$

The energy comes from the mechanical work done to move the rod.
