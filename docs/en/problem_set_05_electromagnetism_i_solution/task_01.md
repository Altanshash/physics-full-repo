# Problem Set 5  
## Electromagnetism I: Field and Forces

---

# Problem 1 – Potential and Energy

## Given

A point charge:

$$
q = 4 \ \mu C = 4 \times 10^{-6} \ C
$$

Distances:

$$
r_1 = 0.3 \ m
$$

$$
r_2 = 0.6 \ m
$$

Test charge:

$$
q_0 = 2 \ \mu C = 2 \times 10^{-6} \ C
$$

Coulomb constant:

$$
k = 9 \times 10^9 \ \frac{N \cdot m^2}{C^2}
$$

---

## 1. Electric potential at \( r = 0.3 \ m \)

The electric potential of a point charge is:

$$
V = \frac{kq}{r}
$$

Substitute the values:

$$
V_1 = \frac{(9 \times 10^9)(4 \times 10^{-6})}{0.3}
$$

$$
V_1 = \frac{36 \times 10^3}{0.3}
$$

$$
V_1 = 1.2 \times 10^5 \ V
$$

$$
\boxed{V_1 = 120000 \ V}
$$

---

## 2. Potential difference between \(0.3 \ m\) and \(0.6 \ m\)

Potential at \( r_2 = 0.6 \ m \):

$$
V_2 = \frac{kq}{r_2}
$$

$$
V_2 = \frac{(9 \times 10^9)(4 \times 10^{-6})}{0.6}
$$

$$
V_2 = 6.0 \times 10^4 \ V
$$

$$
V_2 = 60000 \ V
$$

Potential difference:

$$
\Delta V = V_2 - V_1
$$

$$
\Delta V = 60000 - 120000
$$

$$
\Delta V = -60000 \ V
$$

$$
\boxed{\Delta V = -6.0 \times 10^4 \ V}
$$

---

## 3. Work done to move a test charge \( q_0 = 2 \ \mu C \)

Work is:

$$
W = q_0 \Delta V
$$

Substitute the values:

$$
W = (2 \times 10^{-6})(-60000)
$$

$$
W = -0.12 \ J
$$

$$
\boxed{W = -0.12 \ J}
$$

The negative sign means the electric potential energy decreases when the positive test charge moves from \(0.3 \ m\) to \(0.6 \ m\).

---

## 4. Electric field intensity from the derivative of potential

Electric field is related to potential by:

$$
E = -\frac{dV}{dr}
$$

For a point charge:

$$
V = \frac{kq}{r}
$$

Differentiate:

$$
\frac{dV}{dr} = -\frac{kq}{r^2}
$$

Therefore:

$$
E = -\left(-\frac{kq}{r^2}\right)
$$

$$
E = \frac{kq}{r^2}
$$

At \( r = 0.3 \ m \):

$$
E = \frac{(9 \times 10^9)(4 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.3)^2}
$$

$$
E = \frac{36 \times 10^3}{0.09}
$$

$$
E = 4.0 \times 10^5 \ \frac{N}{C}
$$

$$
\boxed{E = 4.0 \times 10^5 \ \frac{N}{C}}
$$

---

## 5. Comparison with Coulomb's law

From Coulomb's law, the electric field of a point charge is:

$$
E = \frac{kq}{r^2}
$$

This is the same result obtained from the derivative of the potential:

$$
E = -\frac{dV}{dr}
$$

So,

$$
\boxed{
E = \frac{kq}{r^2}
}
$$

The electric field found from potential agrees with Coulomb's law.

---

## Final Answer

$$
\boxed{V(0.3 \ m) = 1.2 \times 10^5 \ V}
$$

$$
\boxed{\Delta V = -6.0 \times 10^4 \ V}
$$

$$
\boxed{W = -0.12 \ J}
$$

$$
\boxed{E = 4.0 \times 10^5 \ \frac{N}{C}}
$$

---

## Conclusion

For a point charge, the electric potential decreases as the distance increases.  
The potential difference between \(0.3 \ m\) and \(0.6 \ m\) is negative because the second point is farther from the charge.  
The electric field calculated from the derivative of potential is the same as the electric field from Coulomb's law.
